Research

My work asks a deployment-oriented question: when continual-learning systems fail on real medical images, is catastrophic forgetting the primary problem, or are preprocessing, representation, labeling, and task design limiting the model before forgetting can even be meaningfully assessed?

MSc thesis direction

Preliminary thesis title

Continual Learning for Abdominal Trauma Detection in CT: A Task-Incremental Study of Forgetting, Windowing, and Learnability

The thesis develops and evaluates a staged diagnostic framework for continual medical-imaging systems. Rather than only tuning regularization or replay hyperparameters, the work tests whether the data representation and task granularity are sufficiently learnable for continual-learning comparisons to be valid.

Core methods
Sequential fine-tuning, Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC), Replay, and EWC+Replay.
Testbed
Abdominal CT trauma detection across bowel, extravasation, kidney, spleen, and liver injury tasks.
Diagnostic lens
Average AUROC, transfer, forgetting, representation quality, label granularity, and patient-level aggregation.
Supervision
Dr. Ashery Mbilinyi; committee member Dr. Jaya Prakash Champati.

Learning to Window: Context-Aware HU Adaptation for Continual Trauma Injury Detection in CT Scans

Lead author · MSc thesis research · University of Victoria

Clinical CT viewing commonly relies on fixed Hounsfield Unit windows. This project replaces that fixed preprocessing choice with a differentiable module that learns task-specific window parameters jointly with the diagnostic model.

0.770Average AUROC with learnable windowing
44%Reduction in catastrophic forgetting
3 / 5Tasks with positive forward transfer
Motivation
Fixed window presets may suppress task-relevant contrast and impose a representation bottleneck before continual learning begins.
Approach
A differentiable Learning-to-Window module learns per-task CT intensity mappings within a continual-learning pipeline.
Baseline
Fixed-window continual learning achieved an average AUROC of 0.540 in the reported experiments.
Outcome
The submitted manuscript reports an increase to 0.770 average AUROC, lower forgetting, and positive forward transfer on three of five tasks.
Availability
Paper and implementation links will be added after the review process permits public release.

Diagnostic study
Manuscript in preparation · targeting MICAD 2026

What Limits Continual Learning for Abdominal-Trauma CT Detection? A Diagnostic Study of Forgetting vs. Representation Bottlenecks

Co-author · Medical Computer Vision Lab, University of Victoria

This work studies a related domain-incremental setting where standard continual-learning methods remain near chance. Instead of interpreting every weak result as catastrophic forgetting, the study evaluates whether the representation and coarse patient-level label are themselves preventing the task from being learned.

Benchmarks
Fine-tuning, EWC, Replay, and EWC+Replay under matched training conditions.
Finding
Weak predictive performance is traced primarily to representation quality and coarse labeling rather than to forgetting alone.
Reformulation
A patient-level multiple-instance learning formulation is used to align slice-level evidence with patient-level abdominal-trauma labels.
Contribution
The study offers a diagnostic workflow for deciding whether to improve continual-learning algorithms or first repair the upstream learning problem.

02

Research objectives

Deployment realism

Test whether continual-learning approaches remain credible on heterogeneous, low-prevalence medical data rather than only on simplified benchmarks.

Method diagnosis

Separate forgetting from failures caused by preprocessing, feature quality, label granularity, and insufficient task learnability.

Pipeline improvement

Develop concrete changes—learnable CT windowing and patient-level multiple-instance learning—instead of only retuning existing methods.

Open problems

Identify limitations that should shape future research in continual medical imaging, including evaluation under scarce data and limited compute.
